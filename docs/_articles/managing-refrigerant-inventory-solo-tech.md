For a solo HVAC technician, the refrigerant cylinders sitting in your truck represent both your ability to complete jobs and a significant amount of tied-up capital. Carrying too much inventory means cash sitting in your van instead of your bank account. Carrying too little means driving to the supply house mid-job, burning time and fuel while a client waits. Getting the balance right — and keeping accurate records — is a daily challenge that directly impacts your profitability and your compliance standing.

## What to Stock and How Much

The refrigerants you carry should reflect the systems you actually service, not every type you might encounter someday. Analyze your last three to six months of service calls to identify your high-volume refrigerants.

### The Core Inventory for Most Solo Techs

For residential and light commercial work in 2026, most solo technicians need:

- **R-410A** — Still the dominant refrigerant in systems installed between 2010 and 2024. Carry at least one full 25-pound cylinder and one partial for topping off.
- **R-22 (reclaimed)** — Needed for older systems still in service. Since R-22 is no longer manufactured, reclaimed stock is expensive. Carry a 30-pound cylinder if you regularly service pre-2010 systems.
- **R-454B** — The primary R-410A replacement in new equipment starting in 2025. As the installed base grows, you will need increasing stock. Start with one 25-pound cylinder.
- **R-32** — Used in some mini-split and VRF systems. Carry a cylinder if you service these units regularly.

### Recovery Cylinders

Always carry **at least two empty recovery cylinders** rated for the refrigerant types you handle. Running out of recovery capacity mid-job means you cannot legally complete a refrigerant removal — and you cannot leave a system open. Recovery cylinders for A2L refrigerants (R-454B, R-32) must meet the appropriate pressure ratings and be properly labeled.

### Avoiding Overstock

Refrigerant does not expire, but overstocking creates problems:

- **Capital tied up** in inventory that may sit for months
- **Vehicle weight** affects fuel economy and tire wear
- **Security risk** — refrigerant theft from service vehicles is increasingly common
- **Regulatory changes** can affect the value of your stock

A good rule of thumb: carry enough to handle **three to five typical service calls** without resupply. Review your stock levels weekly and reorder when a cylinder drops below 25% capacity.

## Weight Tracking: The Foundation of Good Inventory Management

Knowing exactly how much refrigerant is in each cylinder prevents two costly problems: running short during a job and losing track of refrigerant for compliance purposes.

### Why Estimates Are Not Good Enough

Many technicians eyeball cylinder weight by lifting them or checking the pressure gauge. This approach is unreliable. Pressure varies with temperature, and your sense of weight is imprecise when you are handling cylinders all day. A cylinder you thought had 8 pounds might actually have 3, and you will not discover the discrepancy until you are mid-charge on a rooftop unit.

### Practical Weight Tracking Methods

- **Weigh cylinders after every job** using a refrigerant scale. Record the weight immediately — not when you get home, not at the end of the week. Immediately.
- **Record the amount added or recovered** for each service call. This data feeds your EPA compliance logs and your inventory records simultaneously.
- **Track by cylinder**, not just by refrigerant type. If you carry two partial cylinders of R-410A, knowing the weight of each one lets you grab the right cylinder for the job.

## Preventing Cross-Contamination

Cross-contaminating refrigerants is one of the most expensive mistakes a technician can make. Mixing even small amounts of incompatible refrigerants ruins the entire batch and can damage equipment. A contaminated recovery cylinder full of R-410A and trace R-22 cannot be reclaimed — it must be destroyed at a significant cost.

### Segregation Best Practices

- **Color-code your cylinders** and storage areas. Use colored tape, labels, or designated rack positions so you can visually verify the correct cylinder before connecting.
- **Never use the same hoses** for different refrigerant types without thorough evacuation. Dedicated hose sets for each refrigerant type are ideal.
- **Label recovery cylinders clearly** with the refrigerant type and date of first recovery. Never recover a different refrigerant into a cylinder that already contains material.
- **Store A2L refrigerants separately** from non-flammable types and ensure they are in well-ventilated areas of your vehicle, following the safety requirements for mildly flammable refrigerants.

### When Contamination Happens

If you suspect contamination, do not guess — use a refrigerant identifier to test the cylinder. If it is contaminated, label it immediately as mixed/contaminated and arrange for proper disposal through your refrigerant supplier or a certified reclamation facility. The cost of proper disposal is far less than the cost of charging a system with contaminated refrigerant and dealing with the resulting compressor failure.

## Inventory Turnover and Cost Management

Refrigerant is one of your largest variable costs. Managing that cost requires attention to purchasing patterns and pricing.

### Buy Smart

- **Track wholesale prices** across multiple suppliers. Prices fluctuate seasonally — refrigerant costs peak in summer when demand is highest.
- **Buy in bulk when practical**, but only for refrigerants you use consistently. A pallet discount on R-410A saves money only if you will use it within a reasonable timeframe.
- **Build relationships with local supply houses.** Consistent purchasing volume often earns you better pricing, priority service, and advance notice of price increases.
- **Monitor reclaimed R-22 pricing carefully.** Prices can swing dramatically. If you service many R-22 systems, locking in a supply arrangement with a reclamation provider can protect your margins.

### Passing Costs Through Accurately

Every pound of refrigerant you put into a system should be invoiced to the client at your established per-pound rate. This requires knowing exactly how much you used — another reason accurate weight tracking matters. If you estimate "about 3 pounds" and actually used 4.5, you just gave away 1.5 pounds of refrigerant.

## EPA Documentation and Chain-of-Custody

EPA regulations require technicians to maintain records of refrigerant purchases, usage, recovery, and disposition. For solo technicians, this paperwork can easily fall behind if it is not part of your daily workflow.

### What You Must Track

- **Purchases**: Date, quantity, type, and supplier for every refrigerant acquisition
- **Usage**: Amount added to each system, tied to the specific service call and equipment
- **Recovery**: Amount recovered from each system, including the disposition of recovered refrigerant
- **Disposition**: Where recovered refrigerant goes — returned to supplier, sent for reclamation, or destroyed

### Making Compliance Routine

## Key Takeaways

- **Stock based on your actual service mix** — analyze your recent jobs to determine which refrigerants and quantities you need.
- **Weigh cylinders after every job** and record weights immediately, not at the end of the day.
- **Prevent cross-contamination** through color-coding, dedicated hoses, and clear labeling of recovery cylinders.
- **Always carry at least two empty recovery cylinders** rated for the refrigerants you handle.
- **Track wholesale pricing** across suppliers and buy strategically to manage your largest variable cost.
- **Invoice every pound of refrigerant** used — accurate weight tracking prevents giving away product.
- **Log refrigerant data at the time of service** to make EPA compliance documentation automatic rather than a separate task.
